iPhone

7 things to know before considering the iPhone platform

1: App Store. The only way to distribute applications for iPhone is through the App Store and iTunes. Users cannot simply download the application from your web site.

2: Restrictions. Apple limits what App Store applications can do. The most important restrictions are:

  • • No background processing, only one application is active at a time.
  • • No way to intercept calls, SMSes or e-mails.
  • • No access to the calendar, call log, SMS log, e-mail database, ringtones.
  • • Have to follow Apple User Interface Guidelines.
  • • No duplication of built-in functionality.
  • • No adult or restricted content.
  • • No potentially offending content, including political sarcasm.

3: Rejection. Your application may be rejected from App Store based on Apple's sole judgement. Usually you are told about the reason, and usually the problem can be fixed and your application resubmitted.

In some cases, however, it's the nature of your application that is considered unsuitable. Some well-known rejected applications include a countdown clock counting time till the end of the republican parlament (rejected as offensive), and a podcast download application (rejected as a duplicate of iTunes, even though it had many unique features).

There is no way to ask Apple if your application will be accepted or rejected without actually building it. Do not invest in any applications at the borderline of what's allowed on the App Store, or be prepared to loose your investment.

(See below for most common reasons for rejection.)

4: Revenue. Apple withholds 30% of your revenue; the remaining 70% are paid to your bank account monthly. You need, however, to undergo a special legal procedure to avoid US taxes from being automatically withheld, otherwise you end up with 49% of the revenues instead of 70%.

5: Subscription. To publish your application on the App Store under your company name, you need to buy a $99/year subscription from Apple.

The application cannot be installed on any iPhone without buying a subscription, so an iPhone developer needs a subscription too. (He can use his client's one, though.) Only look for developers who own an active subscription, others have not done any serious work yet.

6: Testing. You need to test your application very throughly. First, because an application that crashes during Apple's testing gets rejected immediately. It takes about an hour to fix a crash and about a week to resubmit.

Second, because the users who experience a crash or a bug leave negative reviews and give you one-star ratings. You cannot respond to those reviews and you cannot do anything about those ratings. Even after you fix the bug the negative ratings and reviews will continue to affect your sales. Many iPhone developers tell they are suffering from this.

7: Pricing. Every application is either free or paid. $0.99 is the minimum price for paid apps, with other typical prices being $1.99, $2.99 and $4.99. More pricey applications are rare, though some cost as much as $50 (and still sell quite well).

Starting with iPhone OS 3.0 (to be released in June 2009), paid applications can have an in-application store and sell additional items (e.g.: sell more books for a ebook app, sell more weapons for a shooter app, sell 6 more months of subscription for a news app).

There is no way to let users try your application before buying it. Many vendors solve this problem by publishing a free lite version of their app separately.

Commons reasons for rejection

Functionality:

  • Application does not ask for confirmation before sending user's data (e.g. a taken picture) to the server.
  • Application does not display a message like “Loading…” while data (especially UIWebView) is being loaded.
  • When running on iPod Touch, the application shows a button to access a function (GPS, vibration, camera etc) not available on iPods.
  • Network errors are not handled properly: an appropriate message must be displayed (“Network is not available”), must not hang on “Loading…” indefinitely.
  • False claims of missing network connectivity.
  • Too much bandwidth usage over cellular network (GRPS/EDGE). Limit to about 4.5 Mb per 5 minutes of usage.
  • Incompatibility with any of the advertised OS versions. (If you claim to support 2.0, be sure to test on 2.0, 2.1, 2.2 and 2.2.1.)

User Interface Guidelines violation:

  • Keeping UITableView selection for too long: the application must deselect any selected rows immediately. (The blue color is used to express the click event and must not be used to display a permanent selection.)
  • Using UITableCell's detail disclosure button for something other than navigating to another page.
  • Simulating failures of any iPhone components (e.g. simulating a cracked screen).
  • In-place editing of rows inside UITableView (instead of e.g. adding UITextField to each row, you must navigate to another view for editing).
  • Hiding UIWebView toolbar.
  • Display of any functions not implemented in the application (so your free Lite edition must not show any paid-only functions).
  • Different version numbers specified in various places (e.g. Info.plist and iTunes Connect numbers do not match).
  • Version number too fancy (don't even try 0.0.0).

Texts:

  • Usage of “Limited” or “Beta” in application description (all functions of the app should already work properly). Only “Free” or “Lite” is allowed.
  • Incorrect usage of “iPhone Something” instead of “Something for iPhone” (e.g. “iPhone Games” instead of “Games for iPhone”).
  • Inaccurate app description: make sure all advertised functions exist and work.
  • Inaccurate “What's new” description for updates.
  • Political jokes (in the app itself or in the description).

Artwork/icons:

  • The 57x57 application icon is not a reduced-size version of the 512x512 icon for iTunes.
  • Usage of copyrighted Apple artwork.
  • Usage of any icon that resembles iPhone or iPod touch.
  • Standard “Action” icon does something other than opening an action sheet to select an action.
  • Using standard system icons for any purpose other than their indented one.
  • Anything sexual or erotic (not necessary nude: a game with a character in bikini can also be rejected).

If your application requires (some external service) account to operate, you must provide a test account. Make sure the test account works.

Existing icons

While you need a designer to create your application icon, you might be lucky to obtain some of the other graphics cheaper or free.

For some specific functions, iPhone has the icons built-in. Those icons can only be used to invoke the intended functions (or your app will be rejected). See the list of system-provided icons on the Apple Developer Connection web site (note that you'll need to sign up or login, but the membership is free).

A quick guide for a designer

There aren't many designers out there who have prior iPhone experience, so you can often find yourself explaining the basics of the platform to your designer.

Application icon. At the very least every application needs a nice 57x57 full-color icon. This icon represents the application on the main iPhone screen. iPhone automatically adds rounded corders and overlays a “glossy” effect, so even a simple drawing on a solid background will look good.

Another option is to provide a so-called “prerendered” icon, with the rounded corners and the glossy effect already added by the designer.

It is best to open the App Store and look though the icons of popular applications to grasp the idea of the iPhone style.

The application icon also has to be provided in 512x512 variant for the App Store and iTunes. These icons can only differ in size and details, and cannot be severely different (or the app will be rejected).

If you plan to create a free lite version of your app, you are also required to make a slighly different icon for it. A common practise is to add the word “free” somewhere, often on a red background at the bottom of the icon.

Screen size. iPhone screen is 320x480 in portrait mode (480x320 in landscape), however the upper 20 pixels are taken by the status bar (which can be hidden if the developer wants to), which leaves us 320x460 or 480x300 working area.

Many applications have a so-called navigation bar at the top, 44 pixels high in portrait orientation and 32px high in landscape one.

Many applications also have a tab bar or a toolbar at the bottom. The tab bar is used to switch between screens, and is 49 pixels high. The toolbar contains one or more buttons, and is 44/32 pixels high just like the navigation bar.

Toolbar and tab bar icons. You need to design a black-and-white mask (with alpha) about 30x30 px. This mask is automatically applied to a white (inactive) or blue (active) gradient image.

Some examples of the resulting icons can be found in eddit's icon set (use your imagination to restore the original black-and-white masks) and glyphish icon set (which gives a few examples of the masks and the resulting icons).
